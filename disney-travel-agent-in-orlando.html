What does a Disney travel agent in Orlando actually do?

A Disney-focused travel agent plans and books theme-park vacations — park tickets, on-property and off-property hotels, dining and reservations strategy, and increasingly the flights and rental car too. In the Orlando market specifically, that means more than the parks. Orlando is home to both Walt Disney World and Universal Orlando, and tourism here generated a record $94.5 billion economic impact in 2024 (Visit Orlando). A good local agent navigates the whole region — multi-park itineraries, when to stay on-property versus a nearby resort, and how to time a visit around crowds.

The other thing that makes Orlando distinct: scale. Orlando International Airport (MCO) handled 57.21 million passengers in 2024, making it Florida's busiest airport and the 9th busiest in the United States (RoadGenius / MCO data). International travel through MCO grew by nearly 800,000 passengers year over year (Orlando International Airport). That volume is exactly why a good agent earns their keep: the flights, the transfers, and the parks all have to line up.

Are Disney travel agents free to use?

For the client, usually yes. Authorized Disney agencies are paid a commission by Disney, so most charge travelers nothing for standard park-and-hotel planning (Magic Guides). You get expert planning at no added cost because the supplier — not you — pays the agent. Some agencies add a small fee for complex multi-destination trips, but the core Disney booking is typically free to the traveler.

How do I find a good Disney travel agent in Orlando?

The single most important filter: the agency must be an Authorized Disney Vacation Planner, now branded under Disney's EarMarked program. This designation is awarded to the agency, not the individual, and only to agencies that hit Disney's sales and training standards (Magic Guides). Look for the EarMarked badge on the agency's site, and check whether they list a tier — Silver, Gold, Platinum, or Diamond — since higher tiers often unlock better perks. Several top EarMarked agencies are based right in the Orlando area (Magic Guides).

Beyond the badge, ask three questions. Does the agent know Universal Orlando and the wider region, not just Disney? Can they book your flights, off-property hotel, and rental car — or only the park package? And are they a real Florida-registered business? In Florida, anyone selling travel must be covered under the state's Seller of Travel registration with FDACS — a legitimate agency will be (Travel Market Report).

Why do even Orlando locals use a travel agent?

It sounds backward — why hire an agent for parks in your own backyard? Because the parks are a moving target. Pricing, reservation systems, ride access, and seasonal events change constantly, and a local family planning a multi-day, multi-park trip with out-of-town relatives still has to coordinate tickets, dining, and lodging across both Disney and Universal. Locals also travel out of Orlando — and the same agent who books their staycation can book the cruise out of Port Canaveral or the European summer. A good advisor saves time and avoids costly missteps, which is worth it even an hour from the gates.

How much does a Disney travel agent in Orlando earn?

Honest answer: it depends on volume and how much you sell beyond Disney. Start with the wage floor. Nationally, the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics puts the median wage for travel agents at $48,450 and the mean at $50,040 in May 2024 (BLS). That's the employee benchmark the Orlando metro is measured against.

But most Disney agents aren't salaried employees — they're independent contractors earning commission splits. Disney pays agencies a flat 10% on Walt Disney World packages (MainStreet Travel). New agents typically start on a 50/50 split with their host, so a $5,000 package nets you about $250 — roughly 5% in your pocket. The agents who out-earn the BLS wage do it by selling broadly: Host Agency Reviews' 2024 data shows full-time independent advisors averaged $78,940, and hosted advisors with 3+ years averaged $67,256 (Host Agency Reviews).

How to become a Disney travel agent in Orlando

If you're on the other side of this — an Orlando local who wants to be the agent — here's the real path. It's the local version of the broader how to become a Disney travel agent ↗ route, with two Florida-specific steps.

1. Join an authorized (EarMarked) host or franchise agency

You can't sign up with Disney directly. The Authorized Disney Vacation Planner / EarMarked status belongs to the agency, so your first move is to affiliate with one that already holds it (Magic Guides). Orlando is an ideal base — you live in the product and can plan trips you've actually taken.

2. Pass the College of Disney Knowledge

Once your agency registers you on Disney's advisor portal, you complete the College of Disney Knowledge, Disney's free training covering its full portfolio. It's mandatory before you can sell Disney, and most agents finish in a few days (Magic Guides).

3. Get right with Florida's Seller of Travel rule (FDACS)

This is the step most guides skip. Florida is one of a handful of states that regulates travel selling: anyone selling travel must register as a Seller of Travel with the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS). A full registration is $300 per year plus a surety bond of up to $25,000 — but if you work as an independent contractor under a registered host, you typically file as an Independent Agent for just $50 per year and operate under your host's registration and bond (Travel Market Report). Selling without proper coverage can draw fines up to $5,000. For the full state walkthrough, see how to become a travel agent in Florida ↗.

4. Build a business beyond the parks

Certification makes you a Disney agent. Owning your inventory makes you a travel business. Disney clients in Orlando also fly, stay off-property, cruise from Port Canaveral, and take non-Disney trips — and if all you can book is the park package, you hand that revenue to someone else.
