What does a group travel agent do — and why Atlanta?

A group travel agent coordinates travel for 10 or more people moving together: family reunions, church and ministry trips, HBCU and Greek-life weekends, destination weddings, conventions, sports teams, and corporate incentive trips. Instead of everyone booking separately, the agent negotiates a room block with the hotel, manages a rooming list (who's in which room), collects deposits, and absorbs the back-and-forth so the organizer doesn't have to. In exchange, the agent earns commission — and often a comp room or perk for the group leader.

Atlanta is built for this. Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport served 108.1 million passengers in 2024, keeping its title as the world's busiest airport (ATL Airport Chamber). For a group agent, that's the single most important fact in the market: nearly every attendee — even international ones — can reach Atlanta on a direct flight, which makes it dramatically easier to get 40 people to the same place on the same day.

The visitor economy backs that up. Metro Atlanta drew an estimated 51 million visitors in 2024, supporting a roughly $20 billion hospitality industry, according to the Atlanta Convention & Visitors Bureau (Discover Atlanta / ACVB).

Which groups drive Atlanta's market?

A few segments make Atlanta unusually rich for group agents:

  • Family reunions. Atlanta is a perennial reunion city, with venues like Stone Mountain Park and downtown hotel blocks built around large family gatherings (Discover Atlanta).
  • HBCU and Greek-life travel. The Atlanta University Center's homecoming alone draws over 50,000 visitors annually, one of the most economically impactful HBCU events in the South (BlackPulse HQ). Add the Atlanta Greek Picnic and Divine Nine gatherings, and you have recurring, repeatable group demand.
  • Church and ministry groups, conventions, and destination weddings. Atlanta sits in a select first-tier convention category alongside Chicago, Orlando, and Las Vegas — meaning a steady flow of meetings, incentive trips, and the personal travel that clusters around them.

How do you find a good group travel agent in Atlanta?

If you're the organizer, you want three things: someone who has actually run a group your size, who is transparent about deposits and the cancellation window, and who gives you one point of contact instead of a phone tree.

Ask any candidate how they handle the room block (how rooms are held, when names are due, what happens to unsold rooms), how deposits and final payments are scheduled, and what their cancellation and attrition terms are — attrition is the penalty if you book 40 rooms and only fill 30. A real group pro answers those instantly. If they don't have a clean booking system and a written timeline, keep looking.

You don't need a giant agency. Many of the best group agents in Atlanta are independent advisors working under a host agency or on their own platform — which is exactly the path covered in the next section.

How do you become a group travel agent in Atlanta?

Here's the part most people get wrong: Georgia does not require a seller-of-travel license to sell travel. Only four states — California, Florida, Hawaii, and Washington — require seller-of-travel registration (Law Offices of Mark Pestronk). Georgia is not one of them. You can start working with clients without a state travel-agent license.

That said, "no license" doesn't mean "no setup." Two caveats apply in Georgia: if you want to sell travel insurance, you need a limited-lines travel insurance producer license from the state insurance commissioner; and if you book residents of CA, FL, HI, or WA, those states' rules can still reach you. For most Atlanta group agents starting out, neither blocks you from day one — you just need a way to actually book inventory and get paid.

The realistic startup path looks like this:

  1. Register a simple business (an LLC and a business bank account keep group deposits clean and separate from your money).
  2. Get accredited or join a host agency so you have the credentials suppliers require to earn commission. New advisors who want a head start often learn the foundations of becoming a travel agent ↗ before picking a model.
  3. Choose your inventory and booking engine — this is the real unlock. You can't run a group trip on consumer travel sites; you need wholesale rates and the ability to hold and map rooms.
  4. Pick a niche. In Atlanta, reunions, HBCU/Greek weekends, and faith-based group travel are obvious, high-repeat lanes.

What do travel agents in Atlanta earn?

Be honest with yourself about year one: it's mostly learning, and the income follows experience. Nationally, the median wage for travel agents was $48,450 in May 2024 (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics) — that figure reflects employees, not independent commission earners.

Independent income is a different curve. In Host Agency Reviews' 2024 survey, full-time hosted advisors with three or more years of experience earned $67,256 on average, and independently accredited advisors earned $78,940 (Host Agency Reviews). The same survey shows brand-new advisors earn very little at first — the gap between year one and year three is enormous. Group travel helps you climb that curve faster, because one 40-room reunion can equal dozens of individual bookings in commission.
