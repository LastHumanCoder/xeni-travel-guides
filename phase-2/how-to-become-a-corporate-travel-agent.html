How to Become a Corporate Travel Agent: The 2026 Career Guide

For aspiring corporate agents who want the real path into business travel, not the leisure version with a suit on.

Here's the short answer on how to become a corporate travel agent: learn a global distribution system (GDS) like Sabre or Amadeus, get hired by a travel management company (TMC), usually as a salaried agent, not a commission-only contractor, and learn to run a company's travel program against its T&E policy and duty-of-care obligations. Corporate travel is a different job from booking honeymoons. This guide covers the skills, certifications, what you'll earn, and the choice between the employee and independent paths.

What does a corporate travel agent actually do?

A corporate travel agent books and manages travel for a company's employees, flights, hotels, rental cars, and rail, inside the rules that company sets. That's the core difference from leisure: you're not selling someone their dream vacation, you're executing business trips efficiently, on policy, and on budget.

The work centers on a few things a leisure agent rarely touches. You enforce a travel and expense (T&E) policy, the company's rules on cabin class, hotel caps, preferred suppliers, and approvals. You manage duty of care, the employer's legal and ethical responsibility to protect traveling employees from foreseeable risk; when every booking flows through one managed channel, the TMC and travel manager get a live, global view of where every traveler is at any moment (BCD Travel). And you do account management, being the dedicated point of contact for one or more corporate clients, handling rebookings, reporting, and the 2 a.m. flight cancellation.

It matters because the stakes are large. Global business travel spending rose to $1.47 trillion in 2024 and is forecast to hit $1.57 trillion in 2025, with the U.S. the single largest market at $395.4 billion (GBTA). That's the pool corporate agents work in, and it rewards people who can run a program, not just make a booking.

How is corporate travel different from leisure travel?

The honest version: almost everything about the day-to-day is different.

You get paid differently. Leisure agents live on supplier commission. Corporate agents are usually salaried employees of a TMC or earn through service/transaction fees, because corporate clients treat fees as a normal cost of doing business, "the corporate side, they just know it's part of doing business" (Host Agency Reviews). That's why corporate advisors generally out-earn their leisure counterparts.

You book more air, and you book it in a GDS. Corporate travel is air-heavy, complex multi-city itineraries, last-minute changes, fare rules, and that work happens in a global distribution system, not a consumer website. The GDS is "the main source of inventory for flight distribution," giving agents access to 400+ airlines plus hotels, rail, and car rentals in one terminal (AltexSoft).

You serve a company, not a consumer. Your "client" is an account with a policy, a budget owner, and reporting expectations, not a couple planning an anniversary. To get the job, you usually need to already know the tools, which creates the industry's classic catch-22: "in order to sell corporate travel, a travel agent needs to have experience selling corporate travel" (Host Agency Reviews).

What skills and certifications do you need?

GDS proficiency (the non-negotiable)

Sabre, Amadeus, and Travelport are the three dominant systems, and corporate hiring managers expect fluency in at least one (AltexSoft). You learn them through self-paced GDS training modules, typically priced from roughly $139 to $475 per module through providers like IATA and Travel Leaders of Tomorrow, or on the job at a large TMC that trains its hires on air (Host Agency Reviews). Sabre's own foundational courses run about 40–60 hours, with most beginners finishing basic certification in 8–12 weeks.

Industry certification: GBTA's GTP

The recognized credential in corporate travel is the Global Travel Professional (GTP) certification from the Global Business Travel Association. It's a 125-question exam covering four domains, Strategic Business Planning, Buyer/Supplier Relations, Travel Program Administration, and Data/Analytics/Finance, and the certification is valid for three years (GBTA). It's aimed at people who already have business-travel experience, so it tends to be a mid-career credential rather than a day-one one.

The soft skills that actually decide your success

Policy judgment, calm under pressure, attention to detail, and account-management instincts. Corporate clients judge you on how you handle the disrupted trip at midnight, not the smooth one. Most TMC roles list only a high-school-or-equivalent education requirement and train the rest (BLS).

How much do corporate travel agents make?

Real numbers, and a clear caveat: it depends on whether you're a TMC employee or running your own corporate accounts.

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics puts the median annual wage for all travel agents at $48,450 in May 2024, with the lowest 10% under $33,280 and the top 10% above $74,160 (BLS). Corporate agents typically sit above that median because business travel is higher-ticket and fee-supported, Glassdoor's national average for a corporate travel agent runs to roughly $62,000 a year, above the all-agents median.

There's also a credential premium. GBTA has reported that certified GTP holders earn meaningfully more than non-certified peers with similar experience, one reason the certification holds up as a mid-career investment (GBTA). And the ceiling rises sharply for independents who own client relationships: Host Agency Reviews' 2024 data shows full-time independent advisors averaged $78,940 across all niches (Host Agency Reviews).

The trade-off is real, though: the salaried TMC path gives you stability and benefits but caps your upside; the independent path is uncapped but you have to find the accounts and the technology yourself.

The step-by-step path to becoming a corporate travel agent

1. Learn a GDS

Pick Sabre or Amadeus (they dominate U.S. corporate desks) and complete a certification course. This is the single highest-leverage thing you can do, it's the literal entry requirement most TMCs screen for (AltexSoft).

2. Get hired by a TMC (the employee path)

Large TMCs train new agents on air booking and corporate workflows, which is the cleanest way to break the experience catch-22 (Host Agency Reviews). Start on a service desk, learn the policy-and-duty-of-care side, and build the account-management muscle.

3. Master T&E policy and duty of care

Learn to book within client rules, manage approvals, and run the risk-management side. This is what separates a corporate agent from a leisure one, and it's why centralized booking matters: it's the foundation of a defensible duty-of-care program (BCD Travel).

4. Earn the GTP (when you qualify)

Once you have a few years in, the GTP certification formalizes your expertise and raises your earning power (GBTA).

5. Decide: stay employed, or go independent

If you want to own accounts, especially small and mid-sized businesses that big TMCs underserve, you'll need your own booking infrastructure.
