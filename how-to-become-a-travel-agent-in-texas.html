Do you need a license to be a travel agent in Texas?

No. Texas is not a "seller of travel" state, so there is no state-level registration, bond, or license required to sell travel to Texas residents. Only four U.S. states — California, Florida, Hawaii, and Washington — require seller-of-travel registration (Host Agency Reviews). Texas isn't one of them.

That said, "no license" doesn't mean "no setup." A few things still apply:

  • If you sell travel insurance, the Texas Department of Insurance requires a limited travel insurance license for the entity offering it — though most new agents sell insurance through their host agency's existing license, so you rarely need your own (Texas Department of Insurance).
  • You still need a legal business: register your business name, get an EIN, and keep clean books. None of that is travel-specific.
  • Selling to out-of-state clients? If a client lives in California, Florida, Hawaii, or Washington, that state's seller-of-travel rules can apply to the sale regardless of where you're based. Most host agencies hold these registrations on your behalf — one more reason to work under one.

What about certification?

Certification is optional in Texas, but it builds trust and skill. The Travel Institute offers the entry-level TRIPKit and the Certified Travel Associate (CTA) credential; many host agencies bundle free onboarding training when you join. Certification won't get you "licensed" — there's no such thing in Texas — but it does make you better at selling, which is what actually grows income. How much do travel agents make in Texas?

Let's separate two very different numbers: what employed agents earn, and what independent agents earn.

Employed travel agents in Texas earn a mean annual wage of $47,950, with roughly 3,300 agents on payroll across the state — making Texas the 5th-largest travel-agent employment market in the country, behind Florida, California, Washington, and New York (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, May 2023). Nationally, the occupation employs about 58,250 people at a $50,040 mean wage (BLS, Travel Agents 41-3041).

But most people asking how to become a travel agent in Texas don't want a salaried desk job — they want to work for themselves. That's a different income picture entirely.

Independent (hosted) agents are paid on commission, so the range is wide. In the Host Agency Reviews 2024 survey of 2,300+ advisors, the average hosted advisor earned $52,636, while full-time hosted advisors with 3+ years of experience averaged $67,256 (Host Agency Reviews). First-year income is usually modest — your first year is mostly learning the systems and finding clients. The agents who break $60K+ are the ones who picked a niche and booked consistently. For the full breakdown, read how much travel agents make ↗.

Texas adds one quiet advantage to all of these figures: no state income tax. Texas is one of only a handful of states that levies no personal income tax, and sole proprietorships — the most common structure for home-based agents — are exempt from the state's franchise tax (Texas Comptroller). Every commission dollar you earn is taxed federally, not by the state, which meaningfully improves take-home pay for a home-based business.

Why Texas is a strong market to sell travel

Texas isn't just license-friendly — it's one of the best travel markets in the country to build a client base in.

  • 31.3 million people call Texas home, making it the second-most-populous state and the fastest-growing in the nation by raw numbers (Texas Tribune / U.S. Census). More residents means more honeymooners, families, and corporate travelers near you.
  • DFW International is the 3rd-busiest airport in the world, moving 87.8 million passengers in 2024, with Houston's IAH adding another major international hub (DFW Airport). Easy connectivity makes Texas clients easy to send anywhere.
  • The Port of Galveston is the 4th-busiest cruise homeport in the U.S., hitting a record 1.5 million cruise passengers in 2024 (Port of Galveston). For Texas agents, cruises out of Galveston are a built-in, drive-to specialty — no flights for clients to book.

A growing population, two global airport hubs, and a top cruise port within driving distance is about as good a home market as a new agent can ask for.

The 6 steps to become a travel agent in Texas

1. Pick a niche. Disney, cruises (Galveston is right there), honeymoons, corporate, luxury, group travel. Specialists earn more than generalists because referrals compound.

2. Choose a host agency. A host agency gives you industry accreditation (an IATA/CLIA number), supplier commission agreements, training, and — crucially — coverage for those other states' seller-of-travel rules. Most new Texas agents start hosted rather than going fully independent. Learn what one is and how to choose in our guide to what is a host agency ↗.

3. Register your business. Set up a sole proprietorship or LLC, grab an EIN, and open a business bank account. No travel license needed — just standard Texas business basics.

4. Get trained and (optionally) certified. Take your host's onboarding, then consider The Travel Institute's CTA to sharpen your skills.

5. Get your booking tools. This is the step most guides skip — and it's the one that decides whether you actually earn. You need access to bookable inventory at rates that leave you a margin, plus a way for clients to book and pay you.

6. Market and book. Tell everyone you know, build a simple branded website, niche down your content, and book your first trips. Income follows volume.
