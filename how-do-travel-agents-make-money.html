How Do Travel Agents Make Money? Commissions, Fees, and Markups Explained

For aspiring and new travel agents who want to understand exactly where the income comes from, before they sell a single trip.

So how do travel agents make money? In short, four ways: commissions paid by suppliers, service or planning fees charged to clients, markups on wholesale (net) rates, and bonuses like volume overrides. Most agents blend several of these. The mix matters more than people think, because commission tables are shrinking, while the levers you actually control (fees and markups) are where the real upside now lives. This guide breaks down each income stream with real, cited numbers, then shows where the biggest earnings lever has quietly moved.

How does travel agent commission work?

A travel agent commission is a cut of a booking's value that the supplier, a hotel, cruise line, or tour operator, pays you for selling their product. You don't add it on top of the customer's price; it's baked in. As Expedia Group puts it, "a 'gross rate' is the total price the customer pays... a 'net rate' is the price with the commission already removed, so the difference between the customer price and the net rate is your profit" (Expedia Group).

That distinction, commissionable vs. net, is the whole game. With a commissionable rate, the supplier sets the price and pays you a fixed percentage. With a net rate, the supplier hands you a wholesale price and you decide what the customer pays. The first caps your earnings at the supplier's table. The second doesn't.

Here are the four ways agents actually get paid:

  1. Supplier commissions, a percentage of each booking, paid after travel (or after final payment for cruises).
  2. Service and planning fees, charged directly to the client for your time and expertise.
  3. Markups on net rates, the spread between a wholesale price and what you charge.
  4. Overrides and bonuses, extra commission larger agencies earn for hitting volume goals (Host Agency Reviews).

What commission rates do travel agents earn by supplier?

Rates vary widely by product type. Here's what the data shows for 2026.

Hotels and car rentals

Standalone hotel and car-rental bookings "typically pay travel agents 10% commission," according to Host Agency Reviews (Host Agency Reviews). In practice, hotel commissions run roughly 10–15% depending on the brand and your agency's agreements. For corporate hotel and car bookings, the figure is often lower, around 8–10% (Host Agency Reviews).

Cruises

Cruises remain one of the few categories where traditional commissions are still healthy. Cruise-line commissions "range from 10–20% of the total cruise fare, with an average of around 16% for ocean cruises" (Gateway Travel). CLIA members can also earn bonus commissions, for example, an extra $50 per stateroom on oceanview-or-higher cabins for sailings of seven days or longer (Gateway Travel). One catch: cruise fares include non-commissionable fees (port charges, taxes), so your percentage applies only to the commissionable base, not the headline price.

Packages, tours, and insurance

Vacation packages and tours generally pay more than standalone hotels, and travel insurance is among the highest-commission products of all. The pattern is consistent: bundled and specialized products carry richer margins than commodity bookings like flights.

Flights

Air is the cautionary tale. Domestic airline commissions sit at roughly 0–5%, and even international fares only reach about 10–22% (Host Agency Reviews). Because airlines slashed agent commissions decades ago, most advisors now charge a service fee to book flights instead, a median of about $35 for domestic tickets and $50 for international (Host Agency Reviews).

Why service fees and markups now matter more than commissions

For years, "commission" and "travel agent income" were synonymous. That's changing fast, and the data is unambiguous.

The rise of the service fee

In 2017, only 33% of advisors charged a fee. By 2023, that had jumped to 49% of hosted advisors and 67% of independent advisors, and by 2024 roughly 63% of advisors reported charging fees (Host Agency Reviews). The American Society of Travel Advisors (ASTA) has formally endorsed the practice, with its corporate EVP noting ASTA "has long supported charging professional fees, as recognition of the quality, trusted work they do for clients" (Travel Weekly).

This shift is driven largely by the loss of supplier commissions, especially from airlines, as agencies seek more stable, predictable income (Travel Weekly). And it pays: hosted advisors who charged fees earned, on average, 42% more than peers who didn't in 2024 (Travel Weekly).

Why markups on wholesale rates are the quiet winner

Here's the lever most new agents overlook. When you book a commissionable rate, the ceiling is set by the supplier's table, 10% on a hotel, period. When you access a wholesale net rate, you set the customer price yourself. The "commission" isn't a fixed percentage handed down to you; it's whatever spread you choose to add.

This is the same model online travel agencies have used for years to build margin. For an independent agent, marking up net rates is one of the cleanest ways to grow income, because it isn't capped, and because the rate the customer sees is yours, not the supplier's (Host Agency Reviews). How host agencies, splits, and overrides fit in

Most new agents don't hold supplier contracts on their own, they work under a host agency that has them. The host negotiates higher base commissions, volume bonuses, and supplier perks, then splits the commission with you.

A typical split runs from 60/40 to 80/20, with the higher share going to the agent (Gateway Travel). Experienced, high-volume agents can push that to 90/10 or even keep 100% on a monthly-fee plan. Through a host, base commissions on most bookings land around 10–15%, rising to 20–30% for agents with the right credentials or sales volume (Gateway Travel).

On top of splits sit consortia overrides, bonus commissions a large buying group earns for steering volume to preferred suppliers, then shares with members. They're real money, but they reward scale, so they matter most once you're producing consistently (Host Agency Reviews).

The honest trade-off: a host gives you instant access to inventory and rates you couldn't get alone, but you give up a slice of every booking and you're still tied to supplier commission tables. The way to break that ceiling is to control the rate yourself.
