Do you need a license to be a travel agent in Florida?

There is no "travel agent license" in Florida the way there is for real estate or insurance. But Florida is one of the few states with a mandatory registration: anyone who sells, promotes, or offers travel to the public must register as a Seller of Travel with the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS) under Chapter 559, Part XI of the Florida Statutes. Selling without registering can draw administrative fines of up to $5,000 per violation, plus cease-and-desist and injunctive relief (Gay & Jones / FDACS overview).

That sounds heavy. The good news: the rule is built so that independent agents working under a registered host agency rarely have to carry the full registration themselves.

What does FDACS Seller of Travel registration actually cost?

A full Seller of Travel registration runs $300 per year, plus a performance surety bond of up to $25,000 (the bond premium is typically about 1% of coverage for applicants with good credit). If you sell "vacation certificates," the fee rises to $400 and the bond to $50,000 (Travel Market Report).

There are two important off-ramps. Agencies in continuous compliance for five or more years can apply to waive the bond, and businesses that have held an Airlines Reporting Corporation (ARC) appointment for three-plus years under the same ownership can register as exempt with a statement of exception from FDACS (SuretyBonds.com guide).

Are independent agents under a host agency exempt?

Largely, yes — and this is the part that matters most for a brand-new agent. If you work as an independent contractor under a registered host agency, you typically register as an Independent Agent for just $50 per year, operating under your host's Seller of Travel number rather than filing your own $300 registration and bond (Travel Market Report). The trade-off: as an independent agent you generally can't collect a fee, commission, or other consideration directly from travelers — payment flows through your host. For most new agents booking commissionable hotels, cruises, and packages, that's exactly how the business works anyway.

How to become a travel agent in Florida: the 5 steps

You don't need a college degree or years of experience. The path mirrors the general how to become a travel agent ↗ route, with one Florida-specific step (FDACS) added. Here's the real order.

1. Pick a host agency (or platform)

A host agency gives you the credentials, supplier relationships, commission payouts, and — in Florida — the Seller of Travel coverage you need to operate legally. Independent agents who join a host are by far the most common route into the business; the median hosted advisor has about four years in the field (Host Agency Reviews). Learn what a host agency actually does ↗ before you sign, and compare what each one charges versus what it unlocks.

2. Get trained and (optionally) certified

Florida doesn't require a certification, but training pays off fast. Free supplier academies (cruise lines, Disney, tour operators) teach you to book real products, and bodies like The Travel Institute offer structured certification for agents who want a credential and deeper skills. Start booking simple trips while you learn — competence comes from reps, not just coursework.

3. Register correctly with FDACS

If you're independent under a host, file your $50 Independent Agent registration and operate under their Seller of Travel number. If you're launching your own agency, budget for the $300 registration plus the bond. Either way, this step is non-negotiable in Florida — do it before you promote a single trip.

4. Choose a Florida-friendly niche

Florida hands you natural specialties. You're at the doorstep of the world's busiest cruise market — PortMiami, Port Canaveral, and Port Everglades move millions of cruisers a year — plus Orlando's theme-park machine. Cruises, Disney and Universal packages, multigenerational family trips, and Caribbean getaways are all niches you can sell to neighbors and to clients nationwide.

5. Get a way to book and get paid

This is the step beginners underestimate. Credentials let you call yourself an agent; inventory and a booking engine are what let you actually sell and keep commission. You need access to hotels, flights, and activities at rates that leave you margin — and a clean way to take payment.

How much do travel agents make in Florida?

Honest answer: it's a wide range, and your first year is mostly learning. Nationally, the median wage for employed travel agents was $48,450 in May 2024 (BLS OEWS). But most successful agents aren't salaried employees — they're independents earning commission. In Host Agency Reviews' 2024 survey, full-time hosted advisors with 3+ years of experience averaged $67,256, and independently accredited full-timers averaged $78,940; brand-new advisors, by contrast, averaged just $12,057 as they built their book (Host Agency Reviews).

Florida sweetens the math in two ways. First, demand is concentrated here: in the Miami–Fort Lauderdale–West Palm Beach metro, travel agents are employed at roughly 4.2 times the national rate (BLS, Miami area OEWS). What makes Florida a great place to sell travel?

Few states are better positioned. Florida is the cruise capital of the world — PortMiami, Port Everglades, and Port Canaveral anchor a year-round cruise economy, giving you a steady supply of sailings to sell and clients who book them annually. Orlando's parks, the Keys, the Gulf beaches, and Caribbean proximity round out a deep, sellable backyard. The state's tourism volume also means a dense network of suppliers, FAM trips, and supplier reps who actually show up.

The home-business climate helps too: no state income tax, a low barrier to launching an LLC, and a large population of transplants and retirees who travel often. The one thing to respect is the Seller of Travel rule — handle that correctly (usually via your host) and Florida is one of the most agent-friendly markets in the country.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a license to be a travel agent in Florida?+
There's no occupational "license," but Florida law requires you to register as a Seller of Travel with FDACS. Most independent agents are covered under their host agency's registration and file only the $50 Independent Agent registration.
How much does it cost to become a travel agent in Florida?+
If you join a host agency, expect roughly $50 for Independent Agent registration plus your host's fees. Launching your own agency means the $300 FDACS registration plus a surety bond of up to $25,000 (the premium is usually about 1% of coverage).
Can I become a travel agent in Florida from home with no experience?+
Yes. No degree or prior experience is required. You sign with a host agency, complete free supplier training, register correctly, and start booking — many Floridians run it as a home-based business.
Who is exempt from Florida's Seller of Travel registration?+
Independent agents under a registered host (who register as Independent Agents instead), businesses with a 3+ year ARC appointment under the same ownership, and agencies in compliance for five-plus years may qualify for a bond waiver.
How long does it take to start earning?+
You can book your first trip within weeks of signing with a host. Meaningful income usually builds over the first one to two years as your client base and repeat bookings grow.
