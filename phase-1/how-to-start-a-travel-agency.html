How to Start a Travel Agency in 2026: A Step-by-Step Guide

For the founder ready to build a real travel business, not just sell a trip on the side, but launch an agency you can scale.

Starting an agency is more achievable than it looks, and cheaper too. Most hosted agents launch for a median of just $1,500 (Host Agency Reviews). Knowing how to start a travel agency means stacking eight things in the right order: a business plan, a legal entity, state registration where it applies, supplier access through accreditation, insurance, inventory, a booking-capable website, and your first clients. Skip the order and you waste money on tech before you can legally take a payment. This guide walks the full build.

What are the steps to start a travel agency?

Start with a one-page plan, then form the legal entity, then plug into supplier inventory, in that sequence. The plan forces you to pick a niche (honeymoons, luxury, corporate, groups) before you spend a dollar, because your niche decides which suppliers, accreditations, and clients you chase. A generalist "I sell all travel" agency competes with everyone; a focused one ("destination weddings in the Caribbean") wins referrals faster.

Your plan doesn't need 40 pages. Define your niche, your target client, how you'll reach them, your commission model, and a 12-month cost budget. That last item matters because new advisors in their first year earn modestly, HAR's 2024 survey put new hosted advisors at an average of $12,057, with two-year advisors earning more than double that (Host Agency Reviews). Treat year one as the build, not the payday.

How do I choose a legal structure?

For most new agencies, an LLC is the default. The U.S. Small Business Administration describes the LLC as a hybrid that gives you "the limited liability features of a corporation and the tax efficiencies and operational flexibility of a partnership", your personal assets like your house and savings are shielded if the business fails (U.S. Small Business Administration). You'll register with your state (usually the Secretary of State), appoint a registered agent, and get an EIN tax ID (U.S. Small Business Administration). Budget a small state filing fee, often under $200 depending on your state.

Do I need a license or state registration to start a travel agency?

There is no national travel-agent license. But four states require seller-of-travel registration, and the penalties for skipping it are real if you operate there. The five are California, Florida, Hawaii, and Washington (Host Agency Reviews).

Two examples set the range. California requires every seller of travel to register with the Attorney General's office and display a CST# on all consumer advertising; the registration fee is $100 per location annually, and in-state agencies also pay into a consumer restitution fund (California DOJ). Florida requires annual registration through the Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS), and unlike California it demands a surety bond (FDACS). If you live in one of the other 45 states, you generally have no seller-of-travel filing, but note that California and Florida rules can reach you if you actively sell to consumers there, so check before you advertise across state lines.

How do I get supplier access and accreditation?

You can't earn commission until suppliers recognize your agency, and that recognition comes through an accreditation number. There are three you'll hear about, and one shortcut.

  • ARC (Airline Reporting Corporation) handles money between U.S. agencies and airlines.
  • IATAN is the U.S. arm of IATA; its number is recognized by virtually all travel suppliers but carries the most requirements. IATAN accreditation costs $247 for a head office plus a $103 annual service fee (altexsoft).
  • CLIA is the cruise-focused option with a far simpler application, useful if you're not ticketing air through a GDS (altexsoft).

The shortcut most new agencies take: join a host agency and sell under its accreditation number. You share the host's ARC/IATAN/CLIA credentials, skip the documentation and experience hurdles, and often earn higher commission splits because you're booking under a high-volume ID (altexsoft). What insurance does a travel agency need?

Carry errors and omissions (E&O) insurance before you book a client. It's professional liability coverage that protects you against claims of booking mistakes, bad advice, or supplier defaults, and it pays for legal defense. For U.S. travel professionals, E&O typically runs $400 to $1,200 per year (CCRA). Some host agencies include E&O coverage in their fees, which is one more reason the hosted route lowers your real startup cost. General liability is worth adding once you have an office or meet clients in person.

How much does it cost to start a travel agency?

Less than most people expect, if you go hosted. Host Agency Reviews' 2024 survey put hosted-agent startup costs at a median of $1,500 and an average of $3,478, ranging $500–$5,000 (Host Agency Reviews). Going fully independent can run up to $20,000 once you add your own accreditation, tech, and marketing, and a retail franchise with a storefront can reach $39,000 (Host Agency Reviews).

Your line items: state LLC filing (often under $200), seller-of-travel registration if applicable ($100–$300+ plus a possible bond), E&O insurance ($400–$1,200/yr), accreditation or host fees, and your technology. Choosing a platform that bundles inventory, booking, CRM, and payments, instead of buying each separately, is the single biggest lever on that tech line.

How do I get my first clients?

Pick one niche and one channel, then go deep. Your network is the fastest first source, friends, family, and their referrals trust you before any reviews exist. Build a professional branded website that can actually take a booking, claim your social profiles in your niche, and ask every happy traveler for a referral and a review. As you grow, content (destination guides, planning tips) and a simple email list compound over time. The agencies that grow fastest pair a tight niche with a site that converts browsers into paid bookings on the spot, which loops back to your tech stack.
