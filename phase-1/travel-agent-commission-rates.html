Travel Agent Commission Rates by Supplier: The 2026 Reference

A definitive, cited rate table for every supplier type (cruises, hotels, packages, insurance, Disney and more), plus how splits and markups change what you actually keep.

Most suppliers pay travel agents a commission somewhere between 5% and 40% of the booking, and the exact number depends almost entirely on what you sell. Travel agent commission rates are highest on cruises, all-inclusive packages and travel insurance, and thinnest on airfare and car rentals. This is a rate reference, not an earnings overview: below is the real percentage each supplier type pays, verified against CLIA, ASTA, Host Agency Reviews and supplier documents, plus how a host split and net-rate markup change your take-home from those numbers.

What are travel agent commission rates by supplier?

Commission is a percentage of the commissionable fare that a supplier pays the selling agent after a trip is booked and paid. It is not a flat industry figure. Rates cluster by product category, and the spread is wide: a cruise can pay three to four times what an airline ticket does. Here is where each category lands in 2026.

Cruises: 10-16% (average ~16% on ocean)

Cruises are the classic high-commission product. Rates "typically range from 10% to 16% of the total cruise fare" (Gateway Travel), and the average "hovers around 16% for ocean cruises" (MainStreet Travel). One catch matters enormously: commission is paid only on the commissionable fare, not the total price. Non-commissionable fares (NCFs) such as port charges strip out a chunk before the percentage is applied. An ASTA analysis found that what looks like a 15% cruise rate "is probably closer to 11.7% in practice," with NCFs cutting effective commission by two to three points (Late Cruise News / ASTA).

Hotels: 10-15% (preferred programs average ~12%)

Standalone hotels and resorts pay between "8% and 15% of the room rate," with major chains like Marriott paying "10% commission" (MainStreet Travel). Agents booking through a strong preferred program do better: Fora advisors "average 12% per booking on hotels," a rate driven by preferred partnership agreements rather than published retail commission (Fora Travel).

Vacation packages and all-inclusives: 10-15%+

Bundled packages and all-inclusive resorts sit at the higher end of the hotel range and often beat it, because the percentage applies to the client's entire package: flights, room, meals, drinks and activities included. All-inclusive resorts "typically pay a minimum of 10%, with overrides at certain booking volumes" (Fora Travel), and because everything is commissionable, the dollar payout on a $6,000 family package dwarfs the same rate on a bare room.

Travel insurance: 25-40%

Insurance is the single highest-rate line most agents touch. Commission runs "20% to 37% depending on the provider," and some agencies report "60% of the supplier commission" flowing through on top (MainStreet Travel). Attaching a policy to every trip is one of the most reliable ways to lift average commission per booking without selling anything new.

Disney: flat 10%

Disney is unusual: it pays "a flat 10% commission to all agents for Walt Disney World and Disneyland bookings" regardless of volume or agency (MainStreet Travel). There are no volume overrides on the parks, which is why Disney specialists lean on ticket, dining and package add-ons to grow the ticket size rather than chasing a higher rate.

Tours: 10-20%

Tour operators pay base commissions "between 10% and 20%," with specialized and small-group tours reaching "20% or more" (MainStreet Travel). Guided and escorted tours behave like packages: the whole trip is commissionable.

Car rentals: 5-10%

Car rentals are a convenience add-on, not a profit center. Rates run "5-10% commission," with suppliers like Budget and National paying "5% commission on base rates" only (MainStreet Travel).

Air: 0-5%

Airlines are the reason the "book it yourself" myth persists. Domestic tickets pay "0-5% commission," international fares "10-22%," and carriers like American Airlines pay a flat "$2 per segment" (MainStreet Travel). How does a host agency split change your take-home?

The rates above are what the supplier pays. If you work under a host agency, you keep only your share of that commission. The split "typically ranges from 70/30 to 90/10 in the agent's favor" (Host Agency Reviews). So a 16% cruise commission on a $4,000 fare is $640 to the agency, but on a 70/30 split you take home $448, and on 90/10 you take $576.

Here is the counterintuitive part: a lower split on a strong host can out-earn a higher split on a weak one, because the host's preferred rates raise the base commission first. A well-connected host earning 15% on a hotel, even after an 80/20 split, nets you 12%, beating an unaffiliated agent earning a raw 10% (Gateway Travel). Tiered and preferred-supplier rates

Two forces push your effective rate above the base numbers. First, tiered commissions: many suppliers and hosts raise your percentage as annual sales grow, and cruise lines in particular offer "increased rates or bonuses" at higher booking volumes (Gateway Travel). Second, preferred-supplier programs: hosts and consortia negotiate above-retail rates plus overrides for steering volume to partner brands, which is exactly why preferred hotel commissions average around 12% instead of the published 10% (Fora Travel). New agents typically start at the bottom of each range and climb as they prove volume.

Net rate and markup vs. commission

Commission is not the only way to get paid. On a net rate model, the supplier gives you a wholesale price with no commission built in, and you add your own markup to set the retail price the client pays. The spread between net and retail is your margin, and unlike commission, you control it. If a wholesale hotel room nets at $200 and you sell it at $250, your $50 is a 25% margin, well above the 10% published commission on the same room.

How to negotiate higher commission rates

You can move your effective rate up without switching what you sell:

  1. Concentrate volume with preferred suppliers. Overrides and tier bumps reward loyalty; spreading bookings thin forfeits them.
  2. Join a host with strong preferred deals. The host's negotiated base often beats anything you'd get solo, even after the split (Gateway Travel).
  3. Ask for tier reviews. Once you cross a volume threshold, request the higher band rather than waiting for it.
  4. Attach high-rate lines. Add travel insurance (25-40%) and tours to every eligible trip to lift blended commission.
  5. Use net rates where you can. A markup model sidesteps the negotiation entirely because you set the price.

When is travel agent commission paid?

Commission is almost always paid after the client travels, not at booking. Advisors are typically paid "approximately 30-90 days after their client completes their trip," with timing varying by supplier (Fora Travel). Cruise lines usually pay after the client has paid the fare in full and sailed. This lag is why cash flow, not headline rate, trips up many new agents, and why a service fee at booking is common on low- or slow-paying lines.
