How to Become a Cruise Travel Agent: The CLIA Path, Commission &amp; 2026 Guide

For anyone who loves cruising and wants to turn that passion into a real business selling sailings to clients.

Here's the short version of how to become a cruise travel agent: you join a host agency, get access to cruise-line commissions through a CLIA membership, learn to match clients to the right ship and itinerary, and earn a percentage of every cruise fare you book. You don't need a college degree or a state license in most places, you need product knowledge, a way to get paid, and inventory you can actually sell. This guide walks the real path, the honest income, and where the smart money is going next.

What does a cruise travel agent actually do?

A cruise travel agent (the industry prefers "cruise advisor") is a specialist who books cruises on behalf of clients and earns a commission from the cruise line. You handle cabin selection, dining and excursion add-ons, travel insurance, and the dozens of small decisions that overwhelm first-time cruisers, guarantee vs. assigned cabin, which deck, drink packages, transfers.

It's a real specialty for a reason. Cruise lines reward agents because advisors sell more, and clients clearly value them: 75% of cruise travelers said travel agents have a meaningful impact on their decision to cruise (Travel Market Report). And the market is huge, the global cruise industry carried 34.6 million passengers in 2024 and is projected to reach 37.7 million in 2025 and 41.9 million by 2028 (CLIA 2025 State of the Cruise Industry). Demand is growing faster than the supply of advisors who really know the product.

Do you need a license or certification to sell cruises?

No state requires a "cruise agent license." A handful of states (California, Florida, Washington, Hawaii) require seller-of-travel registration for travel sellers generally, if you live in one, you register through your host agency or directly. What the cruise world does have is a professional credential most serious advisors pursue: the CLIA path. It isn't legally required, but it's how you unlock cruise-line commissions and recognition.

The CLIA certification path, step by step

CLIA, the Cruise Lines International Association, is the trade body for the cruise industry, and its individual-agent program is the de facto credential for cruise advisors. Here's how the path actually works.

Step 1, Join a host agency

You almost never sell cruises as a solo, unaffiliated person. You work under a host agency: an established agency that holds the supplier accreditations, collects commission from cruise lines, and splits it with you. Crucially, CLIA Individual Agent Membership requires you to be affiliated with a CLIA Travel Agency Member or Premier Agency Member, essentially a credentialed host agency (AltexSoft / CLIA). Picking a host is your first real decision. (New to the concept? Start with what a host agency is and how it works ↗.)

Step 2, Get your CLIA membership and EMBARC ID

Once you're under a qualifying agency, you apply for CLIA Individual Agent Membership (IAM). Annual dues are $139 for agents at a Travel Agency Member ($89 at a Premier Agency Member) (AltexSoft / CLIA). Approved members get the CLIA EMBARC ID, the card that gives you industry recognition, partner discounts, training, and access to cruise-line agent rates and commissions.

One honesty note: CLIA recommends applicants have generated at least $5,000 in cruise commissions in the prior 12 months before enrolling, and your host makes the final call on supporting the application (AltexSoft / CLIA). In practice that means you often start selling under your host's accreditation first, then earn your own CLIA card.

Step 3, Earn your certifications

CLIA offers a five-level progression you climb as you sell and study (CLIA Certification):

  • CCC, Certified Cruise Counsellor (entry-level, for agents new to cruise)
  • ACC, Accredited Cruise Counsellor
  • MCC, Master Cruise Counsellor
  • ECC, Elite Cruise Counsellor (top individual credential)
  • TAE, Travel Agency Executive (for agency leaders)

Each level combines coursework, ship inspections, and sales/experience requirements. The CCC is where you begin, it covers cruise fundamentals and sales technique and signals to clients you've been trained.

How cruise commission works (and what you'll really earn)

This is the best news about the cruise niche: commissions are typically higher than other travel categories, and they're paid on the cruise fare, a big-ticket item.

How much commission do cruise agents make?

Cruise commissions generally run 10% to 16% of the commissionable cruise fare, scaling with your booking volume (Gateway Travel). On a $4,000 balcony cabin for two, 14% is $560 in commission for a single booking, before add-ons like pre-booked shore excursions, which can pay another 8–12%.

But that gross commission gets split with your host. Suppliers pay the host agency, which then splits with you, a common starting split is 70/30 (you keep 70%), often climbing to 80/20 as you produce more (Fora Travel). So on that $560, a new agent on a 70/30 split nets about $392.

What does that add up to per year?

Be realistic. Government wage data covers travel agents broadly: the median annual wage for travel agents was $48,450 in May 2024, with the lowest 10% under $33,280 and the top 10% over $74,160, and those figures include commission income (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics). Independent, hosted advisors tell a similar story: full-time hosted advisors with 3+ years of experience averaged $67,256, while those in their first three to five years averaged about $44,127 (Host Agency Reviews).

The pattern is clear: your first year is mostly learning, and income compounds with experience and repeat clients. Cruise is a relationship business, happy cruisers rebook for years.

The smartest cruise agents don't only sell cruises

Here's the move that separates a hobbyist from a business owner: a cruise rarely travels alone. Cruisers fly to the port, need a pre-cruise hotel night, want airport transfers, and often tack on a post-cruise city stay or excursions. In fact, CLIA reports the majority of cruise passengers spend one or more nights in a hotel around their sailing, that's revenue sitting right next to your cruise booking.

If all you can book is the cruise itself, you hand the flight, the hotel, and the activities to someone else (or to the client's own browser). Broaden your inventory and you capture the whole trip, and diversify beyond a single niche.
