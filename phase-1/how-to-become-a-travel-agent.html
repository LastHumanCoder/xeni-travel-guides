How to Become a Travel Agent in 2026: A Step-by-Step Guide

For aspiring agents who want the honest path, from "do I need a license?" to your first paid booking.

If you are wondering how to become a travel agent, here is the short version: in most of the United States, you do not need a license or a degree to start. You join a host agency for access to inventory and an IATA number, do a little training, pick a niche, and start booking trips for commission. The longer version matters, though, because the order you do these steps in (and the tools you choose) decides whether you actually earn money or just collect a business card. This guide walks the full path, with real numbers.

What does a travel agent actually do today?

A modern travel agent, increasingly called a "travel advisor", plans, books, and manages trips on behalf of clients, and earns a commission from suppliers (hotels, airlines, cruise lines, tour operators) for the sales. You are not competing with Expedia on price; you are selling expertise, time saved, and access to rates and perks that travelers cannot easily find alone.

The role is smaller and more specialized than it was 20 years ago, but it is steady. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics counts about 65,700 travel agent jobs and projects roughly 2% growth from 2024 to 2034, with around 7,100 openings each year (BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook). Most new entrants today are independent or home-based, which is exactly why this guide focuses on that path.

Do you need a license or certification to be a travel agent?

No, there is no federal license for travel agents, and most states require nothing to start. As the home-based travel agent training resource puts it bluntly, "there is no federal or state licensing of travel agents" (HomeBasedTravelAgent.com).

There is one exception worth knowing: four states require "seller of travel" registration, California, Florida, Hawaii, and Washington. (Iowa repealed its requirement on July 1, 2020; New York only requires registration for agencies with a physical storefront.) These rules apply if you sell to residents of those states, regardless of where you are based. A few specifics:

  • California, register for a Seller of Travel number and join the Travel Consumer Restitution Fund (about $330).
  • Florida, annual registration (around $300) plus a bond, filed with the Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services.
  • Hawaii and Washington, registration with the state, sometimes with a surety bond.

If you operate under a host agency (more on that next), the host often holds the relevant registration, simplifying compliance. Either way, confirm your own state's rule before you sell.

Is certification required?

No certification is required by law in the U.S. (Host Agency Reviews). It is optional, and useful for credibility. The most common entry-level credential is The Travel Institute's TAP (Travel Agent Proficiency) test, which costs $95 and works like a final exam for a "Travel Agent 101" course (The Travel Institute). For a deeper professional credential, the ASTA Verified Travel Advisor (VTA) program runs nine self-paced online courses covering ethics, compliance, and consumer protection (ASTA).

Should you join a host agency or go independent?

This is the most important early decision. A host agency is an established travel company that lets independent agents book under its accreditation, its IATA number, in exchange for a share of your commission. An IATA number is the seven-digit identifier suppliers use to verify you are a legitimate agent before they pay you. Getting one yourself is slow and demanding; under a host, you book on day one and still keep your own commission (Host Agency Reviews).

The trade-off is the commission split. A common rule of thumb is to look for at least a 70/30 split, you keep 70%, though some hosts charge a flat fee instead, or set booking quotas. For almost every new agent, a host is the right call: it provides the accreditation, supplier relationships, training, and back-office that would take years to build alone. Here are the seven steps, in order:

  1. Check your state's seller-of-travel rule (most have none; CA/FL/HI/WA do).
  2. Choose a host agency and read the commission split and fees carefully.
  3. Complete onboarding/training, most hosts include it free.
  4. Pick a niche so you become the obvious choice for a type of traveler.
  5. Get inventory and a booking platform so you can actually sell.
  6. Set up a simple branded website and start marketing.
  7. Make your first booking and collect commission.

How should you pick a travel niche?

Generalists struggle; specialists get referred. A niche makes your marketing sharper and your expertise believable. Popular, high-value niches include:

  • Cruises, the cruise industry's trade body, CLIA, represents 50+ cruise lines and offers agent membership plus certification, with over 29,000 CLIA Certifications awarded in North America (CLIA).
  • Luxury and adventure travel, the highest-earning leisure niches, per income surveys.
  • Disney and theme parks, honeymoons and destination weddings, group travel, and corporate travel (typically the highest-paid of all).

Pick something you genuinely know or want to learn deeply. Your niche will shape which suppliers and inventory you rely on most.

How much do travel agents make?

Honestly? It depends heavily on your path and your effort, and your first year is mostly learning, not earning. Here is what the real data shows.

The BLS reports a median wage of $48,450 for employed travel agents (May 2024), with the lowest 10% under $33,280 and the top 10% above $74,160 (BLS). But that excludes the self-employed, where the upside lives. The Host Agency Reviews 2024 income survey found full-time hosted advisors averaged $67,256, and full-time independently accredited advisors averaged $78,940, while salary-only employees averaged $49,947 (Host Agency Reviews).

The catch: newer agents earn far less while they build a book. Full-time agents with 3–5 years averaged around $44,127, rising past $79,000 only after 15+ years. For the full breakdown by experience and niche, read how much travel agents make ↗.

How do travel agents actually get paid?

Almost entirely by commission. When you book a hotel, cruise, or tour, the supplier pays a percentage, typically 10–16% depending on the supplier, after the client travels. If you work under a host, that commission is split per your agreement (the 70/30 rule of thumb). Some agents also add a planning or service fee for complex itineraries.

The practical implication: your income scales with how much you book and what margin you keep. That is exactly why your booking platform matters, wholesale rates plus your own markup control means more of each sale stays with you.
