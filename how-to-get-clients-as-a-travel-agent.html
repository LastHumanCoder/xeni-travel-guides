How do travel agents get their first clients?

Your first clients almost always come from people who already trust you — your personal network — plus one sharp decision about who you serve. Demand is on your side: an ASTA survey found 50% of travelers say they're more likely to use a travel advisor now than before, and 68% agree that planning a trip has gotten more complex (ASTA, via TravelAge West). People want help. Your job is to make sure the right ones find you.

1. Niche down so you're the obvious choice

"I book travel" is forgettable. "I plan multigenerational Disney trips" or "I do honeymoons in the Maldives" is referable. A niche makes your marketing specific, your expertise believable, and word of mouth easy — friends repeat "she only does cruises" far more readily than "she books trips." Picking a niche also tells you exactly where to show up (the Facebook groups, the hashtags, the local events) instead of shouting into the void. Not sure this career fits yet? 2. Mine your warm network first

Before any paid marketing, tell everyone you know — personally and specifically. Not a generic "I'm a travel agent now" blast, but targeted messages: "You mentioned an anniversary trip — that's exactly what I plan now. Want me to put together some options?" Your first ten clients are usually friends, family, coworkers, and the people they immediately think of. Deliver for them, and the referral flywheel starts turning.

How do travel agents get more clients online?

Once your network is warmed up, online channels scale your reach — but only if you give prospects somewhere credible to land and an easy way to act.

3. Build a professional booking website

A social profile gets attention; a website earns trust and closes the sale. This matters because buyers research before they commit: 75% of consumers always or regularly read online reviews, and 71% won't even consider a business rated below three stars (BrightLocal). When a referral Googles your name, a real site — with your niche, your packages, and the ability to actually book — converts curiosity into a client. A site that only displays a contact form leaks the people who were ready to buy.

4. Show up on social media with content, not ads

Social platforms are where trips are dreamed up: 75% of travelers use social media to research and find inspiration for their next trip, and 48% have booked a trip based on a social media post (Statista). You don't need to go viral — you need to be consistently useful in your niche. Post real itineraries, honest hotel reviews, packing tips, and client wins (with permission). The goal is for someone to think "I should ask her" the moment travel comes up.

5. Claim and optimize your Google Business Profile

When someone searches "travel agent near me" or your name, you want to own that result. A free Google Business Profile puts you on Maps and in local search, and it's where many of those review-readers will judge you: Google is the #1 place consumers read reviews (81%), and 88% say they'd use a business that responds to all of its reviews (BrightLocal). Fill it out completely, add your niche, post updates, and reply to every review — good or bad.

How do you keep clients coming back?

Acquisition gets expensive fast. The agents who win build a machine where every happy client becomes two more — through referrals and repeat trips.

6. Make referrals systematic, not accidental

Referrals aren't luck; they're a process. Ask at the peak moment — right after a client returns raving — and make it specific: "If you know anyone planning a honeymoon, I'd love an introduction." The payoff is enormous because word of mouth is the most trusted channel in travel. Beyond the 37% who chose their agent on a friend's recommendation, real-world agencies report that the majority of new business comes by word of mouth, with top agents at nearly 100% (Travel Weekly). 7. Turn one trip into a lifetime of trips

Repeat clients are the cheapest clients you'll ever have — and travelers are loyal when you earn it: 41% of people happy with their agent rebook without even checking alternatives (Travel Weekly). The math is compelling, too: travelers who use an advisor take 4.7 trips a year versus 3.6 for those who don't (ASTA, via TravelAge West). Capture every client's details — anniversaries, kids' ages, dream destinations — and reach out before they start planning. This is where a CRM stops being optional.

8. Use email and reviews to stay top of mind

Email is the highest-ROI channel you fully own — no algorithm in the way. A simple monthly note with a timely deal, a destination spotlight, or a "now's the time to book summer" nudge keeps you in front of past clients and warm leads. Pair it with a habit of requesting a review after every trip: those reviews are what the next prospect reads, and fresh, plentiful reviews are exactly what consumers look for before they trust a business (BrightLocal). For more channel-by-channel tactics, ASTA's advisor resources are a solid reference (ASTA).
