What Is a Host Agency? A Travel Agent's Guide to Splits, Fees &amp; How to Choose

For new and aspiring travel agents who want the credentials and support to start selling, without the cost of building an agency from scratch.

A host agency is an established, accredited travel agency that lets you sell travel under its umbrella, using its IATA/CLIA accreditation, its supplier deals, and its booking tools, in exchange for a cut of your commission. Instead of spending years and thousands of dollars getting accredited on your own, you plug into a host and start booking almost immediately. It's why 93% of new travel advisors chose the hosted route in 2024 (Host Agency Reviews). This guide explains exactly what a host provides, how splits and fees work, how to choose one, and where modern technology now fits in.

What does a host agency actually do?

The simplest way to think about it: a host agency is a parent company that gives independent travel advisors the infrastructure to run a business, while letting them stay self-employed. You operate as an independent contractor under the host's umbrella, you control your schedule, your clients, and your niche, but you book through the host's credentials and systems (ASTA).

Here's what a good host agency provides:

  • Accreditation you can borrow. Booking flights, cruises, and hotels for commission requires an industry credential, an IATA/IATAN, ARC, or CLIA number. Getting your own is expensive and slow. With a host, "these agents use their host agency's accreditation number, they do not need to have their own accreditation," so you skip the financial and administrative burden entirely (Host Agency Reviews).
  • Supplier access and higher commissions. A host pools the sales of hundreds or thousands of agents, which earns it preferred status and richer commission rates than any solo agent could negotiate. Agents using a host "typically receive a higher commission level with most vendors than they would on their own" (Host Agency Reviews).
  • Commission tracking and payments. The host chases down, collects, and disburses your commissions, a genuine headache to manage alone.
  • E&O insurance. Most reputable hosts include errors-and-omissions (professional liability) coverage in their fees, or offer it as an affordable add-on under the host's policy.
  • Tech and booking tools. GDS or booking-engine access, a CRM, marketing templates, and supplier portals usually come bundled.
  • Training and community. Onboarding, supplier certifications, and a peer network, invaluable when you're new.

Host agency vs. independent: what's the difference?

An independent (independently accredited) agent holds their own IATA/ARC number and keeps 100% of every commission, but they also pay for accreditation, tools, insurance, and supplier relationships themselves, and they have to earn volume-based rates alone. A hosted agent trades a slice of commission for instant access to all of that. The data shows why most beginners start hosted: the median hosted advisor has just 4 years of experience, versus 13 years for independents (Host Agency Reviews). Hosting is the on-ramp; going independent is often a later move once your volume justifies it.

How do host agency commission splits work?

This is the number everyone asks about. When you book a trip, the supplier pays a commission (say, 10–16% of the booking). Your host keeps a share and pays you the rest. That ratio is the commission split, and it typically ranges from 70/30 to 90/10 in the agent's favor (Host Agency Reviews). On a 70/30 split, you keep $700 of every $1,000 in commission; on 90/10, you keep $900.

Many hosts use tiered splits that rise with your annual sales. At Fora, for example, the split starts at 70% to the advisor, climbs to 80/20 once you book $300,000 in trailing-12-month sales, and reaches 90/10 at $2 million in annual sales (Fora Travel). Why a higher split isn't always more money

The biggest mistake new advisors make is comparing hosts on split alone. Because a host negotiates better base commission rates than you could, a lower split on a stronger host can pay you more in real dollars. Picture a hotel booking: an unaffiliated agent might earn 10%, while a well-connected host negotiates 15%, so even after an 80/20 split, you'd pocket 12%, beating the solo agent (Gateway Travel). Commission-recovery speed, supplier access, and support quality often matter more than the headline percentage.

What does a host agency cost?

Hosts charge a mix of monthly and/or annual fees, sometimes plus the commission split. Across the industry, annual fees "vary from free to join... up to $500/year," and some hosts add setup or transaction fees (SquadTrip). The all-in cost of getting started is modest compared to building your own agency: per Host Agency Reviews' 2024 survey, hosted advisors' startup costs ran an average of $3,478, a median of $1,500, and a most-common figure of just $500 (Host Agency Reviews).

What about earnings? Among full-time advisors with 3+ years of experience, hosted advisors averaged $67,256 and independently-accredited advisors averaged $78,940 (Host Agency Reviews). How to choose the best host agency for travel agents

There are roughly 200 host agency listings to sort through, so a structured comparison beats gut feel (Host Agency Reviews). Weigh these factors:

  1. Real take-home, not just the split. Model what you'd actually keep given the host's negotiated supplier rates, not the advertised percentage.
  2. Fee structure vs. your volume. A low monthly fee suits a part-timer; a higher fee with a richer split can pay off at volume.
  3. Niche and supplier fit. If you sell cruises, prioritize CLIA strength and cruise-line relationships; for Disney, an authorized planner program; for luxury, consortia like Virtuoso.
  4. Training and support. Especially critical in year one. Read host profiles and at least 10 member reviews, watching for comments on commission-recovery speed and support responsiveness.
  5. Technology. What booking engine, CRM, and marketing tools are included, and do they let you build your own brand, or just resell theirs?
