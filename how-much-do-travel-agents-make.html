How Much Do Travel Agents Make? A Real Look at Travel Agent Salary in 2026

For anyone weighing a travel career: the honest numbers, not the brochure version.

So, how much do travel agents make? The short answer: the median U.S. travel agent earns about $48,450 a year, but that single figure hides almost everything that matters. Employees and independents earn very differently, your first year is usually rough, and the people pulling six figures got there by selling more, not by waiting for a raise. This guide walks through the real travel agent salary data from the BLS and the largest industry income survey, so you know what's likely, what's possible, and what actually moves the number.

What is the average travel agent salary?

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics puts the median annual wage for travel agents at $48,450 as of its most recent data, with a mean closer to $50,040 (BLS OES, SOC 41-3041). Median means half earn more, half earn less.

The spread is what's interesting. The bottom 10% of travel agents earn well under $35,000, while the top 10% earn more than $74,160 a year (BLS OES). That top figure is a ceiling for employees on a payroll, but as you'll see below, it's nowhere near the ceiling for independents who control their own commissions.

One more thing the BLS number quietly tells you: it counts people with W-2 jobs. It does not capture the large and growing population of self-employed and host-agency-affiliated advisors, who tend to earn more and aren't fully reflected in payroll surveys.

Is the travel agent job market shrinking?

You'll hear that "the internet killed travel agents." It didn't, but it did change the job. The BLS projects travel agent employment to grow about 2% from 2024 to 2034, slower than average, with roughly 7,100 openings per year as people retire or switch careers (BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook). The role that's growing fastest isn't the storefront employee, it's the independent advisor who sells complex, high-touch, personalized trips that travelers don't want to book alone.

Employee vs. independent: why the gap is so big

Here's where "how much do travel agents make" splits into two very different answers.

Employees earn a salary (often plus a small commission share) and that's roughly the $48,450 BLS world. Predictable, capped, and usually with benefits.

Independent and host-agency advisors earn commission. Their income tracks how much travel they sell, not what a pay band allows. According to Host Agency Reviews' 2024 Travel Advisor Survey, the industry's largest, with over 2,000 advisors reporting, the picture looks like this:

  • Full-time hosted advisors (working under a host agency ↗, with 3+ years of experience) earned about $67,256 on average.
  • Full-time independently-accredited advisors earned about $78,940 on average (Host Agency Reviews, 2024 survey).

Both numbers sit well above the BLS median. Why? Independents averaged about 13 years of experience versus 4 for hosted advisors, and they keep a far larger share of what they sell (Host Agency Reviews). Commission income simply isn't pinned to a salary table.

The honest part: your first year is hard

If you only read one section, read this one. The averages above describe established advisors. New advisors earn a fraction of that.

In the same Host Agency Reviews data, advisors with fewer than three years in the business earned an average of about $12,057 in their reporting year (Host Agency Reviews, Hosted Advisor Report 2024). And in earlier ASTA-cited research, 80% of advisors in business two years or less reported earning under $25,000 a year (American Society of Travel Advisors).

This is not a scam or a sign you're bad at it. It's the shape of a commission business. Your first year is mostly building: learning suppliers, getting your first clients, earning trust, and waiting on commissions that pay after travel completes, sometimes months after you book. Treat year one as an apprenticeship that happens to pay a little, not as your income baseline.

The good news is the ramp is steep once it starts. Host Agency Reviews found full-time hosted advisors with 3–5 years of experience earned 87% more than those with two years (Host Agency Reviews). Experience compounds, because repeat clients and referrals do the selling for you.

Full-time vs. part-time: most agents do this on the side

A large share of travel agents work part-time, especially early on, and the income reflects the hours. Part-time advisors in the Host Agency Reviews survey typically earned somewhere in the $20,900 to $30,951 range depending on experience (Host Agency Reviews).

That's a feature, not a bug. Many advisors keep a day job, sell travel evenings and weekends, and scale up only once the commissions justify going full-time. Because the work is largely remote and you set your own volume, "how much do travel agents make" is partly a question of how much you choose to sell.

How commission actually drives independent income

This is the mechanism behind every number above. When you book travel, the supplier (hotel, cruise line, tour operator) pays a commission, commonly around 10% of the booking. If you work through a host agency, you split that commission with the host. A standard arrangement starts around 70% to the advisor, scaling up to 80% and even 90% as your annual sales grow (Fora Travel).

The math makes the path obvious. ASTA gives a clean example: two advisors both earn 10% on a 70% split, but one books a $15,000 trip and the other books $3,000. The first nets $1,050; the second nets $210 (American Society of Travel Advisors). Same effort to make the sale, five times the income. That's why niche and average trip value matter as much as volume, and why luxury, cruise, and group specialists out-earn generalists.

There's also a timing reality worth naming. Most commissions pay after the client travels, not when you book, so a trip you sell in spring for fall travel can mean waiting until winter to get paid. Cash flow lags effort, which is another reason year one feels thin even when you're busy.

This is also exactly where the income ceiling lives. Standard commission income is capped two ways: the supplier sets the commission rate, and the host takes a cut. You're earning a percentage of a percentage that someone else decides, and you only earn it on the products that happen to pay commission, which leaves a lot of travel you can't profit from at all.

So, how much can you realistically make?

Putting the verified data together:

  • Year one: likely under $25,000, often closer to $12,000, treat it as training income (HAR, ASTA).
  • Employee / typical: around the BLS median of $48,450 (BLS).
  • Established hosted advisor: about $67,256 (HAR 2024).
  • Established independent advisor: about $78,940 (HAR 2024).
  • Top earners: six figures and well beyond, driven by volume, high trip values, and keeping more of each sale.
