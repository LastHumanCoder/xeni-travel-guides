How to Become a Travel Agent in Washington State: Your 2026 Step-by-Step Guide

For Washingtonians who want to sell travel from home: legally, profitably, and without waiting months to start.

Here's the short version: to become a travel agent in Washington, you sign with a host agency, get a little training, pick a niche, and start booking. But Washington adds one step most states skip. Because it is one of only a handful of states that regulates travel selling, you (or your host agency) must register as a Seller of Travel with the state. It sounds like red tape. It usually isn't. Most new Washington agents are already covered the moment they join the right host. This guide walks you through every step, the real fees, what agents here actually earn, and how to start booking faster.

Do you need a license to be a travel agent in Washington?

There is no "travel agent license" in Washington the way there is for real estate or insurance, and there is no state exam to pass. What Washington does have is a mandatory registration: under the Sellers of Travel Act (RCW 19.138), any person or business that sells, or advertises to sell, travel services must register as a Seller of Travel before doing business (RCW 19.138.100, Washington State Legislature).

That registration is handled through the Washington State Department of Revenue (DOR), which issues the Sellers of Travel endorsement, while the Department of Licensing answers regulatory questions about the program (Sellers of Travel, Washington Department of Revenue). The heavy sound of "statute" fades fast once you see how the rule is built: it is designed so that agents working under an already-registered agency rarely have to carry the registration themselves.

What does the Washington Seller of Travel endorsement cost?

The Sellers of Travel endorsement runs about $222 per location through the Department of Revenue, covering your main office plus any additional branch you add (Sellers of Travel, Washington Department of Revenue). Registration is renewed annually; under RCW 19.138, each seller of travel renews on or before July 1 every year (Chapter 19.138 RCW, Washington State Legislature).

Registered sellers who collect money from travelers directly also have to safeguard those funds, typically by maintaining a trust account at a federally insured institution until the travel is delivered (Chapter 19.138 RCW, Washington State Legislature). For a brand-new agent, that trust-account obligation is one more reason the host-agency route is so common: your host handles it.

Are agents under a host agency exempt?

Largely, yes, and this is the part that matters most if you are just starting out. Washington law spells out an exemption: an employee, independent contractor, or outside agent does not have to register separately as long as they conduct business in the name of and under the registration of a registered seller of travel, and all money is collected in that registered seller's name and processed by them (RCW 19.138.100, Washington State Legislature).

In plain terms: if you book under your host agency's Seller of Travel registration and let payments flow through the host, you can operate legally without filing your own endorsement or running your own trust account. For most new agents selling commissionable hotels, cruises, and packages, that is exactly how the business works anyway.

How to become a travel agent in Washington: the 5 steps

You don't need a college degree, a license exam, or years of experience. The path mirrors the general how to become a travel agent ↗ route, with one Washington-specific step (the Seller of Travel registration) added. Here's the real order.

1. Pick a host agency (or platform)

A host agency gives you the credentials, supplier relationships, commission payouts, and (in Washington) the Seller of Travel coverage you need to operate legally. Independent agents who join a host are by far the most common on-ramp into the business; the median hosted advisor has about four years in the field (Host Agency Reviews). Learn what a host agency actually does ↗ before you sign, and compare what each one charges against what it unlocks.

2. Get trained and (optionally) certified

Washington doesn't require any certification, but training pays off fast. Free supplier academies from cruise lines, Disney, and tour operators teach you to book real products, and structured programs like The Travel Institute offer a recognized credential for agents who want deeper skills. If you want the full picture of what is and isn't required nationally, see the guide to travel agent licensing and certification ↗. Start booking simple trips while you learn; competence comes from reps, not just coursework.

3. Register correctly under RCW 19.138

If you're independent under a host, you are typically covered by the host's Seller of Travel registration and don't file your own. Just confirm in writing that you're operating under their registration and that payments run through them. If you're launching your own agency, budget for the ~$222 Department of Revenue endorsement per location, the July 1 annual renewal, and the trust-account requirement. Either way, get this squared away before you promote a single trip.

4. Choose a Washington-friendly niche

Washington hands you natural specialties. Seattle is a major Alaska-cruise homeport, so cruises, especially Inside Passage and Alaska sailings, are a repeatable, high-value niche you can sell to neighbors and to clients nationwide. Add the region's outdoor and national-park travel (Olympic, Mount Rainier, North Cascades), Pacific Northwest road trips, and Asia-Pacific gateways out of Sea-Tac, and you've got a deep, sellable backyard.

5. Get a way to book and get paid

This is the step beginners underestimate. Credentials let you call yourself an agent; inventory and a booking engine are what let you actually sell and keep commission. You need access to hotels, flights, and activities at rates that leave you margin, and a clean way to take payment.

How much do travel agents make in Washington?

Honest answer: it's a wide range, and your first year is mostly learning. Nationally, the median wage for employed travel agents was $48,450 in May 2024 (BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook, Travel Agents). Washington sits above that line: BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics data for the state puts the average annual wage for travel agents at roughly $48,480, reflecting the region's high overall pay scale (CareerExplorer, citing BLS OEWS, SOC 41-3041).

But most successful agents aren't salaried employees; they're independents earning commission. In Host Agency Reviews' 2024 survey, full-time hosted advisors with 3+ years of experience averaged $67,256, and independently accredited full-timers averaged $78,940; brand-new advisors, by contrast, averaged just $12,057 as they built their book (Host Agency Reviews).

Washington sweetens the math in two ways. First, the Seattle metro is one of the country's wealthiest, powered by Amazon, Microsoft, and a dense base of high earners who travel often and book premium trips, the kind of clientele that drives bigger commissions per booking. Second, Washington has no state income tax, so a home-based agent keeps more of every commission dollar than a peer in California or New York. Want the full national picture? What makes Washington a great place to sell travel?

Few states pair a wealthy, well-traveled customer base with this much natural inventory. Sea-Tac (SEA) is a top-tier international hub, feeding Asia-Pacific, Europe, and Alaska routes, while Seattle's cruise terminals anchor the Alaska cruise season every summer, a niche clients rebook year after year. The Amazon-and-Microsoft economy means a large pool of high earners for corporate travel, milestone trips, and luxury getaways, and the surrounding region (Olympic and Mount Rainier national parks, the San Juan Islands, wine country, and Vancouver just across the border) gives you sellable trips in every direction.

The home-business climate helps too: no state income tax, a straightforward path to launching an LLC, and a tech-savvy population comfortable booking online. The one thing to respect is the Seller of Travel rule under RCW 19.138. Handle that correctly (usually via your host) and Washington is one of the more rewarding markets in the country to build a travel business.
