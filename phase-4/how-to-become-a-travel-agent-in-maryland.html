How to Become a Travel Agent in Maryland: Your 2026 Step-by-Step Guide

For Marylanders who want to sell travel from home, in a state that is about to regulate it for the first time.

Here's the short version: to become a travel agent in Maryland, you pick a host agency, get a little training, choose a niche, and start booking. For years Maryland had no travel-selling rules at all. That is changing. A new state law, the Don't You Worry (Wurie) Act, takes effect October 1, 2026 and, for the first time, will require sellers of travel and their independent agents to register with the state. Sound intimidating? It isn't, if you understand who it actually covers. This guide walks you through every step, the real costs, what you'll earn, and how to start booking sooner.

Do you need a license to be a travel agent in Maryland?

There is no "travel agent license" in Maryland the way there is for real estate or insurance, and there is no exam to pass. Under current law, travel agents are not licensed or regulated at all (Maryland General Assembly, HB 994 fiscal note). That is what makes Maryland different from a state like Florida, which has required Seller of Travel registration for decades.

But Maryland is now joining the short list of states that regulate travel selling. In May 2026, Governor Wes Moore signed the Don't You Worry (Wurie) Act into law, and it takes effect October 1, 2026 (The Washington Post). The law is named for a Maryland couple who died on a pilgrimage to Mecca after booking through an unlicensed operator, and it is designed to protect consumers from travel fraud.

What does Maryland's new Seller of Travel law require?

Starting October 1, 2026, a "seller of travel" and its "independent agents" must register annually with the Maryland Department of Labor, pay a filing fee, and file proof of professional liability and errors-and-omissions (E&O) insurance of at least $1,000,000 (Maryland HB 994 fiscal and policy note). The initial filing fee is set at $300, and by statute the department can raise fees by no more than 12.5% a year. Each seller of travel also has to file a list of its affiliated independent agents, and no one may collect payment for travel unless those annual filing requirements have been met.

Because the law is brand new, the fine print is still being written. The industry group ASTA has publicly asked Maryland for clarity on exactly how it will apply, so expect the Department of Labor's regulations to fill in the details before and after the effective date (Travel Weekly).

Are independent agents under a host agency exempt?

This is the part that matters most for a brand-new agent, and it is also where Maryland's law is unusually specific. The Act defines an "independent agent" as someone who arranges travel but does not take money directly from the traveler or the supplier, does not hold ticket stock, and cannot issue tickets or travel documents. A "seller of travel," by contrast, is the party that receives payment directly (Maryland HB 994 fiscal note).

In practice, a new home-based agent working under a host agency usually fits the "independent agent" definition, because payment flows through the host, not through you. The law does still require independent agents to carry E&O insurance and to be listed by their seller of travel, so this is not a total exemption the way Florida's $50 independent-agent path is. How to become a travel agent in Maryland: the 5 steps

You don't need a college degree, years of experience, or a state exam. The path mirrors the general how to become a travel agent ↗ route, with one Maryland-specific step (the new registration) that becomes relevant on October 1, 2026. Here's the real order.

1. Pick a host agency (or platform)

A host agency gives you the credentials, supplier relationships, and commission payouts you need to operate, and now, in Maryland, it also helps you meet the new registration and insurance requirements. Joining a host is by far the most common route into the business. Before you sign, read up on what a host agency does ↗ and compare what each one charges against what it unlocks.

2. Get trained and (optionally) certified

Maryland doesn't require certification, and there is no licensing exam. But training pays off fast. Free supplier academies from cruise lines, Disney, and tour operators teach you to book real products, and bodies like The Travel Institute offer structured certification for agents who want a credential and deeper skills (The Travel Institute). Start booking simple trips while you learn; competence comes from reps, not just coursework. If you want the full picture on credentials, see our guide to travel agent licensing and certification ↗.

3. Get your Maryland registration and insurance in order

Before the October 1, 2026 effective date, most agents had no state paperwork at all. Now you will need to be covered under the new Seller of Travel rules: proof of at least $1,000,000 in E&O insurance, the $300 annual filing with the Maryland Department of Labor, and, if you're independent, your listing under a registered seller of travel (Maryland HB 994 fiscal note). A good host agency will help you line this up. Do it before you promote a single trip.

4. Choose a Maryland-friendly niche

Maryland hands you natural specialties. You sit in the DC metro area, surrounded by affluent federal workers, contractors, and professionals who travel often for both work and leisure. BWI Marshall Airport is one of the busiest in the mid-Atlantic, feeding easy access to warm-weather escapes, Europe, and cruise homeports up and down the coast. Corporate and government-adjacent travel, family beach trips to Ocean City and the Delaware shore, Caribbean getaways, and multigenerational Disney vacations are all niches you can sell to neighbors and to clients nationwide.

5. Get a way to book and get paid

This is the step beginners underestimate. Registration lets you operate legally; inventory and a booking engine are what let you actually sell and keep commission. You need access to hotels, flights, and activities at rates that leave you margin, plus a clean way to take payment.

How much do travel agents make in Maryland?

Honest answer: it's a wide range, and your first year is mostly learning. Nationally, the median wage for employed travel agents was $48,450 in May 2024 (BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook, Travel Agents). In Maryland's largest metro, the Baltimore-Columbia-Towson area, travel agents earned a mean of about $42,160 a year across roughly 120 employed agents, per BLS-sourced OEWS figures for May 2024 (VocationalTrainingHQ, citing BLS OES 41-3041).

But most successful agents aren't salaried employees; they're independents earning commission. In Host Agency Reviews' 2024 survey, full-time hosted advisors with 3+ years of experience averaged $67,256, and independently accredited full-timers averaged $78,940, while brand-new advisors averaged just $12,057 as they built their book (Host Agency Reviews).

Maryland sweetens the math on the demand side. The state is anchored to the Washington-Baltimore market, one of the highest-income regions in the country, full of federal employees, contractors, and dual-income professional households who travel frequently and can afford to hand the planning to an expert. What makes Maryland a good place to sell travel?

A few things stack in your favor. First, the customer base: the DC-metro suburbs are packed with affluent, time-poor households that value a real travel planner. Second, the airport: BWI Marshall is a major low-cost and legacy hub, which makes it easy to sell attractive fares to leisure and corporate clients alike. Third, geography: you're a short drive from Atlantic beaches, cruise homeports, and a full slate of family destinations, and a nonstop flight from the Caribbean and Europe.

The one new thing to respect is the registration law that begins October 1, 2026. It is not a barrier so much as a bit of housekeeping: carry the required E&O insurance, file the annual fee, and operate under a compliant seller of travel. Handle that correctly, usually through your host, and Maryland is a genuinely strong market to build a home-based travel business.
