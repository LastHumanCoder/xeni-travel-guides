How to Become a Travel Agent in Indiana: Your 2026 Step-by-Step Guide

For Hoosiers who want to sell travel from home, with low overhead, no red tape, and a fast start.

Here's the short version: to become a travel agent in Indiana, you sign with a host agency, get a little training, pick a niche, and start booking. And unlike Florida, California, or Washington, Indiana adds no extra step. The state does not require Seller of Travel registration and has no travel agent license, so there is nothing to file with the state before you sell your first trip. Pair that with one of the lowest costs of living in the country, and Indiana is quietly one of the best places to run a home-based travel business. This guide walks you through every step, the real costs, what you will actually earn, and how to start booking sooner.

Do you need a license to be a travel agent in Indiana?

No. Indiana has no "travel agent license" the way it licenses real estate agents or insurance producers, and it is not one of the states that requires Seller of Travel registration. Only a handful of states regulate travel selling at the state level. California, Florida, Hawaii, and Washington are the notable ones; Indiana is not among them (Host Agency Reviews). That means no state registration fee, no surety bond, and no waiting on a state agency before you can legally book travel for clients.

That is a real head start. Where a new Florida agent has to work under a registered host's Seller of Travel number just to be compliant, an Indiana agent can focus straight on the parts that actually build a business: training, a niche, clients, and a way to book.

Are there any rules I still have to follow?

Yes, but they are ordinary small-business rules, not travel-specific ones. If you run under your own name, you may operate as a sole proprietor; most agents eventually register an LLC with the Indiana Secretary of State for liability protection, which costs $95 to file online (Indiana Secretary of State, Business Services). You will also want an EIN from the IRS (free) and, if you use a business name, a registered "doing business as" filing. That is the whole list. None of it is unique to selling travel.

What about clients in other states?

This is the one nuance worth knowing. The state registration rules that Indiana skips are triggered by where your client lives, not where you sit. If you sell a trip to someone who resides in California or Florida, those states' Seller of Travel laws can technically apply to that transaction (Travel Weekly, Mark Pestronk). In practice, a good host agency carries the registrations that cover you, which is one more reason new agents almost always start under a host.

How to become a travel agent in Indiana: the 5 steps

You do not need a college degree or prior experience. The path mirrors the general how to become a travel agent ↗ route, minus the state-registration step other states force on you. Here is the real order.

1. Pick a host agency (or platform)

A host agency gives you the credentials, supplier relationships, commission payouts, and the industry accreditation (an ARC or IATA number) that lets you book commissionable travel. Joining a host is by far the most common way into the business; in 2024, 93% of new advisors chose the hosted route (Host Agency Reviews). Learn what a host agency actually does ↗ before you sign, and compare what each one charges against what it unlocks.

2. Get trained and (optionally) certified

Indiana requires no certification, but training pays off fast. Free supplier academies from cruise lines, Disney, and tour operators teach you to book real products, and bodies like The Travel Institute offer structured certification for agents who want a credential and deeper skills (The Travel Institute). Start booking simple trips while you learn. Competence comes from reps, not just coursework.

3. Set up your business the simple way

Because there is no state travel registration, this step is short. Decide on a structure (sole proprietor to start, or a $95 LLC through the Indiana Secretary of State), get a free EIN, and open a separate business bank account so your commission income stays clean at tax time. That is genuinely all the paperwork Indiana asks of you.

4. Choose an Indiana-friendly niche

Indiana hands you a few natural specialties. It is the motorsports capital of the country, and the Indianapolis Motor Speedway alone drives an estimated $1.058 billion in annual economic impact for the state (Indiana University Public Policy Institute, via Indianapolis Recorder). Event and group travel around the Indy 500, the Brickyard, Big Ten sports, and conventions is a real market. So are family trips to Florida and the Caribbean from a state with a cold winter, and cruises out of nearby ports for Midwest travelers who would rather have someone handle the logistics.

5. Get a way to book and get paid

This is the step beginners underestimate. Credentials let you call yourself an agent; inventory and a booking engine are what let you actually sell and keep commission. You need access to hotels, flights, and activities at rates that leave you margin, plus a clean way to take payment.

How much do travel agents make in Indiana?

Honest answer: it is a wide range, and your first year is mostly learning. In Indiana, employed travel agents earned a median annual wage of about $48,530, with roughly 640 travel agents employed statewide, according to BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (BLS OEWS Indiana data, via TradeCareerPath). That is right in line with the national median of $48,450 for travel agents in May 2024 (BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook).

But most successful agents are not salaried employees. They are independents earning commission, and the ceiling is higher. In Host Agency Reviews' 2024 survey, full-time hosted advisors with 3+ years of experience averaged $67,256, and independently accredited full-timers averaged $78,940; brand-new advisors, by contrast, averaged just $12,057 as they built their book (Host Agency Reviews).

Here is where Indiana quietly wins. The state's cost of living runs about 10% below the national average, with housing roughly 23% cheaper (Salary.com, Cost of Living in Indiana). For a home-based agent, that means the same commission dollar stretches further. Your overhead is a laptop and a phone, your rent or mortgage is low, and your margin as a solo operator is stronger than it would be in a high-cost coastal market. Want the full national picture? What makes Indiana a great place to sell travel?

More than you might expect. Start with the demand. Indianapolis International Airport (IND) crossed 10 million passengers in 2024 for the first time in its history, a record driven by the NBA All-Star Game, the total solar eclipse, and the Indy 500 (Indianapolis Airport Authority). A central location and a strong hub airport mean easy connections for your clients to almost anywhere.

Then there is the business climate. Indiana's low cost of living and low cost of doing business make the home-based margins better than almost anywhere on the coasts, and the lack of any state travel license removes the one bureaucratic hurdle other states impose. Add a genuine event-and-motorsports tourism economy that pulls visitors in and sends Hoosiers out, and you have a market with both directions of travel to sell into. The main thing to respect is simply doing the small-business setup properly. Handle that once, and Indiana is one of the most agent-friendly states in the country to build a book from home.
