Here's the short answer: you do not need a license to be a travel agent in most of the United States. There is no federal travel-agent license, and most states require nothing at all. The bigger confusion is mixing up three different things — a license, a voluntary certification, and an industry accreditation number. They sound alike and mean completely different things. Do you need a license to be a travel agent?

No. There is no federal license to be a travel agent, and the federal government does not regulate or certify individual travel agents at all (Host Agency Reviews). You will not find a "travel agent license" exam the way you would for real estate or cosmetology, because no such national credential exists.

What trips people up is the word "license." In the four states that do regulate the business, the requirement is technically a registration, not a license — and it applies to the act of selling travel to that state's residents, not to you personally as an individual. California's own program is explicit about this: registration "does not constitute approval by the State of California" (CA Attorney General). It's a consumer-protection filing, not a stamp of professional competence.

So if you live in, say, Ohio, Texas, or Michigan and sell travel to clients in those states, you need no state credential whatsoever to legally start. That's genuinely the rule — not a loophole.

So what's the catch?

The catch isn't legal — it's practical. Having permission to call yourself a travel agent and having the ability to actually book travel and earn a commission are two different things. You still need supplier access (an accreditation number), and most new agents get that through a host agency rather than applying alone. We'll cover that below. The legal bar is low; the operational bar is where the real setup happens.

Which states require "seller of travel" registration?

As of 2026, four states require seller-of-travel registration: California, Florida, Hawaii, and Washington (Host Agency Reviews). Iowa used to be a fifth, but it repealed its requirement effective June 2020 (Host Agency Reviews). Every other state requires nothing.

One crucial wrinkle: these laws are written to protect consumers in that state. So they can apply to you even if you don't live there. If you're based in Nevada but you sell a cruise to a client in Florida, Florida's rule can apply to that sale. That's why these four states matter to agents everywhere.

California

All sellers of travel must register with the California Attorney General's Seller of Travel Program before operating, must renew annually, and must display their registration number on all advertising (CA Attorney General). The annual fee is $100 per business location, and you must register at least 10 days before offering air or sea travel. California also requires proof of financial security — a trust account, surety bond, or participation in a consumer-protection deposit plan — under Business and Professions Code §17550.15.

Florida

Anyone selling travel to Florida residents must register annually with the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS) (FDACS). The annual fee is $300 (plus $100 if you sell vacation certificates), and a performance bond of up to $25,000 is generally required under Florida Statute §559.929. Selling without registering can carry fines of $5,000 per offense. One useful exemption: if you've held an Airlines Reporting Corporation (ARC) appointment for at least three years with no ownership change, you can request an exemption instead.

Hawaii

Hawaii requires every travel agency to be registered with the Department of Commerce and Consumer Affairs (DCCA) before it sells or advertises travel services, under Chapter 468L of the Hawaii Revised Statutes (Hawaii DCCA). Hawaii registrations renew on a two-year cycle, expiring December 31 of every odd-numbered year.

Washington

Washington requires a seller-of-travel registration through the Department of Licensing, governed by RCW Chapter 19.138 (Washington DOL). The endorsement must be renewed and maintained to keep selling travel legally in the state. (Note: a 2025 rumor that Washington repealed this rule is false — only Iowa has repealed.)

License vs. certification vs. accreditation — what's the difference?

This is the heart of the confusion. Three different things get jammed under "do I need a license," and they serve three different purposes.

A license (or registration) is a government requirement. As covered above, this is mostly N/A — no federal license, and only four states require registration.

A certification is a voluntary professional credential from an industry body. It proves competence to clients and suppliers. Nobody legally requires it, but it builds credibility. The main ones:

  • The Travel Institute offers the entry-level TAP (Travel Agent Proficiency) test — a 100-question proctored exam, 80% to pass — plus the CTA (Certified Travel Associate) for advisors with at least a year of experience, and the advanced CTC (Certified Travel Counselor) requiring five years and a CTA (The Travel Institute).
  • ASTA (American Society of Travel Advisors) offers the Verified Travel Advisor (VTA) certification — a nine-course program, with recertification every two years (ASTA).

An accreditation is the credential that actually lets you book and get paid. It's a supplier-facing number, not a personal license:

  • An IATA / IATAN number (the U.S. arm is the International Airlines Travel Agent Network) is an 8–9 digit code that identifies your agency to suppliers for booking and commission (IATA).
  • An ARC number (Airline Reporting Corporation) handles money flow between airlines and U.S. agencies and is what you need to issue airline tickets directly (IATA).
  • A CLIA number (Cruise Lines International Association), issued via its EMBARC ID, is the cruise industry's accreditation for booking cruises and earning commission (CLIA).

Here's the key insight: most new agents don't apply for these numbers alone. The financial and bonding requirements are steep, so the standard path is to join a host agency ↗, book under its accreditation, and split the commission. That's how the vast majority of independent agents legally book travel without ever applying for an IATA number themselves.

No. Certification is optional and earned over time. Many successful agents start with zero certifications, join a host agency for accreditation, and pursue a TAP or CTA later for credibility. Don't let "I'm not certified yet" stop you from starting.

Frequently asked questions

Do you need a license to be a travel agent in the US?+
No. There is no federal travel-agent license, and most states require nothing. Only California, Florida, Hawaii, and Washington require a "seller of travel" registration, and that applies to selling travel to those states' residents — not to you as an individual (Host Agency Reviews).
What is a seller of travel registration?+
It's a state consumer-protection filing required before you sell travel to residents of California, Florida, Hawaii, or Washington. It typically involves an annual fee and, in some states, financial security like a surety bond. It's a registration, not a competency license (CA Attorney General).
Do I need to be certified to be a travel agent?+
No. Certifications from The Travel Institute (TAP, CTA, CTC) or ASTA (VTA) are completely voluntary. They build credibility but are never legally required. Many agents earn them after they start (The Travel Institute).
What's the difference between a license and an IATA number?+
A license/registration is a government requirement. An IATA, ARC, or CLIA number is a supplier accreditation that lets you book travel and earn commission. They're unrelated — and most new agents access accreditation numbers through a host agency rather than applying alone (IATA).
How much do travel agents make without a license?+
Since no license is required, earnings depend on bookings, not credentials. The median wage for employed travel agents was $48,450 in May 2024, with the top 10% earning over $74,160 (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics). Independent agents earn commission, so income scales with sales volume.
