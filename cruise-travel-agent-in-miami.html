Cruise Travel Agent in Miami: Your 2026 Guide to the Cruise Capital of the World

For Miami travelers who want an expert to handle the whole sailing, and for locals who want to turn that demand into a business.

If you're looking for a cruise travel agent in Miami, you've got more sailings within an hour's drive than almost anywhere on Earth. PortMiami calls itself the "Cruise Capital of the World," and the numbers back it up: it moved 8,233,056 cruise passengers in fiscal 2024 (Miami-Dade County / PortMiami). A good agent doesn't just book the ship, they build the whole trip around it. This guide covers what a Miami cruise agent actually does, what they earn, and how to become one.

Why Miami is the world's busiest cruise hub

PortMiami has topped the global rankings for cruise passengers for most of the last five decades. In fiscal year 2024 it welcomed 8,233,056 cruise passengers, a 12.79% jump over its prior record, and the port openly markets itself as "the busiest one in the world for cruises" and "the cruise capital of the world" (CBS Miami). For fiscal 2025, PortMiami projected growth to roughly 8.56 million passengers (Miami-Dade County / PortMiami).

And Miami isn't the only port in reach. Just up I-95 in Fort Lauderdale, Port Everglades set its own record with 4,010,919 cruise guests in fiscal 2024, a 39% surge year over year after Disney Cruise Line opened a second homeport there (Port Everglades). For a South Florida client, that means an agent can compare departures from two of the planet's biggest cruise gateways without anyone driving far.

The brands live here, too. Carnival Corporation, Royal Caribbean Group and Norwegian Cruise Line are all headquartered in the Miami area, and MSC opened a $100 million North American headquarters west of PortMiami, with plans to add 1,500 jobs (Fox Business). When the people who run the ships choose Miami, the local cruise market runs deep, and so does the demand for agents who know it.

What does a cruise travel agent in Miami do?

A Miami cruise agent does far more than reserve a cabin. The cruise fare is just the spine of the trip. Around it sits a pre-cruise hotel night in Brickell or on the beach, airport-to-port transfers, parking at the terminal, shore excursions in each port of call, travel insurance, and often a post-cruise stay before flying home. The agent assembles all of it into one itinerary the client doesn't have to think about.

That bundling matters because cruisers lean on advisors more than almost any other traveler. CLIA's research shows nearly 80% of cruisers rely on travel advisors for their knowledge, and 73% say an advisor meaningfully influenced their decision to cruise (CLIA). Cabin categories, deck location, dining tiers, drink packages, and itinerary quirks are genuinely confusing, and a Miami agent who has walked the terminals and sailed the ships can steer a client to the right choice fast.

Local knowledge is the edge. A Miami cruise agent knows which terminal a given line uses at PortMiami versus Port Everglades, how early to arrive on a Saturday turnaround, where to park, and which pre-cruise hotels offer cruise-and-park deals. That hyper-local fluency is exactly what an out-of-state booking site can't replicate, and it's the reason the host agency you book under ↗ matters less to the client than the human guiding the trip.

How much do Miami cruise travel agents make?

Honest answer: earnings swing widely with volume and how much of the trip you sell. Start with the baseline. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics puts the median wage for travel agents at $48,450 in May 2024, with the lowest 10% under $33,280 and the top 10% above $74,160 (BLS).

Miami is an unusually dense market for the profession. In the May 2024 wage data, travel agents were employed in the Miami–Fort Lauderdale–West Palm Beach metro at 4.20 times the national rate, one of the highest concentrations in the country (BLS Southeast). That's both an opportunity (a built-in cruise clientele) and a reality check (real competition).

The bigger lever is what you bundle. A cabin commission alone is modest, but cruise lines pay agencies commission on the fare, and the pre/post hotels, flights, transfers, excursions and insurance you add carry their own margin. Sell the whole package and a single sailing can be worth several times the cabin commission. That's why the highest earners aren't the ones who book the most cabins, they're the ones who own the most of each trip.

How to become a cruise travel agent in Miami

Two things matter here: a Florida-specific legal step, and a cruise-specific credential. Neither is hard, but both are real.

1. Register as a Seller of Travel with FDACS

Florida is one of the few states that regulates travel selling. Anyone who sells travel to Florida residents must register annually with the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS) and post a performance surety bond (FDACS). The annual registration fee is $300 (plus $100 if you offer vacation certificates), and new registrants need a $25,000 surety bond (FDACS). Selling travel in Florida without registering, or being properly exempt, can draw fines of $5,000 per offense.

The practical shortcut: most independent agents work under a host agency that holds the full Seller of Travel registration, and the agent files as an Independent Sales Agent tied to that host (FDACS). 2. Build cruise credibility with CLIA

The cruise industry's professional body is the Cruise Lines International Association (CLIA), and the path mirrors the national route to becoming a cruise travel agent ↗. As an Individual Agent Member you receive an EMBARC ID, the number you give clients and suppliers to prove you sell travel, and gain access to CLIA's training (CLIA). From there you can pursue CLIA's certification ladder, which runs from Certified Cruise Counsellor up through Accredited, Master, and Elite Cruise Counsellor designations (CLIA). In a market this competitive, those credentials signal expertise to first-time cruisers choosing whom to trust.

3. Pick your host and your platform

A host agency gives you accreditation, supplier relationships, and a commission split. But the host decides your cruise commissions, not the rest of the trip.
